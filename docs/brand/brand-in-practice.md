# Brand In Practice

This document turns the Reflections brand into surface-level rules. Use it when writing public pages, product UI, AI copy, privacy explanations, Pro prompts, emails, SEO, and launch material.

The narrative principles explain what Reflections believes. This document explains how those beliefs should show up in real product surfaces.

## Website

The public website should explain the product quickly and calmly.

Rules:

- Lead with the specific. A poetic headline is allowed when nearby copy explains the real product.
- Keep paragraphs short. Most visitors scan before they commit.
- Give every public page one clear next action.
- Make privacy, AI, and pricing claims concrete.
- Avoid mission-statement openings unless the page is explicitly a founder note.

Good direction:

> Your mind beautifully organized
>
> A private journal for writing what's on your mind, noticing patterns, and reflecting only when you're ready.

Avoid:

> Transform your life with intelligent emotional analytics.

## Product UI

Product UI should help the user write, save, return, and understand what happened.

Rules:

- Clarity over cleverness.
- Empty states should name what is empty and offer a next step when useful.
- Error states should say what happened, whether writing is safe, and what to try next.
- Loading states should be short and truthful.
- Confirmation dialogs should name the exact thing being changed or deleted.
- Brand expression should come through rhythm, restraint, typography, and humane copy, not decorative noise.

Good:

> No notes yet. Start with a few lines whenever you're ready.

Avoid:

> No data found.

## AI

AI should be explicit, optional, and user-triggered unless a user has clearly turned on a named mode.

Rules:

- Use plain labels such as "Reflect with AI" and "Refresh with AI".
- Say when AI runs and what it uses.
- Frame AI output as a reflection, not a verdict.
- Do not imply the app watches, monitors, diagnoses, or understands the user better than they do.
- If Smart Mode is mentioned, say that the user turns it on.

Good:

> AI runs when you ask for a reflection, refresh your Life Wiki, or turn on Smart Mode.

Avoid:

> Your AI companion continuously understands your inner world.

## Privacy And Legal

Privacy copy should be plain, concrete, and humble.

Rules:

- Name what Reflections stores.
- Name what can leave the device or account.
- Name what the user can export, delete, or turn off.
- Avoid vague reassurance such as "privacy is our top priority" unless the next sentence explains what that means.
- Avoid absolute guarantees such as "completely secure".

Good:

> We do not use your personal notes to train AI models.

Avoid:

> Your data is completely safe with us.

## Pro And Pricing

Pro should feel like more room, not emotional leverage.

Rules:

- Explain access, limits, renewal, and trial timing plainly.
- Do not imply care, healing, privacy, or self-understanding depends on payment.
- Free limits should be calm and exact.
- Upgrade prompts should stay compact.

Good:

> Pro keeps writing unlimited and lets you refresh AI-supported views whenever you ask.

Avoid:

> Upgrade to unlock your healing.

## Emails, SEO, And Social

These surfaces can be slightly more direct than product UI, but they should keep the same restraint.

Rules:

- SEO titles and descriptions should be specific enough to help searchers understand the product.
- Social copy may be warmer or sharper, but should not become generic wellness advice.
- Emails should lead with why the user is receiving them.
- Transactional emails should avoid decorative drama.
- Newsletter copy can have more personality, but should still make one clear point.

Good:

> Reflections is a private journal for writing notes, naming moods, and noticing patterns. AI support runs only when you choose it or turn on Smart Mode.

Avoid:

> The ultimate platform for unlocking your best self.

## Brand Checks Before Shipping

Before publishing a public or product surface, check:

- Does the page make one clear promise?
- Does supporting copy explain the actual product behavior?
- Is AI framed as optional and explicit?
- Are privacy and pricing claims concrete?
- Is there one obvious next action?
- Does any copy pressure the user, diagnose the user, or sell self-understanding through fear?
