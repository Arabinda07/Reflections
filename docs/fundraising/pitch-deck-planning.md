# Reflections Pitch Deck Planning Guide

The deck derives from `docs/fundraising/investment-memo.md`. The memo is the source of truth for the investment case.

## Deck theorem

Investors should believe that Reflections can turn private writing into trusted long-term personal memory, then expand into personal reflection infrastructure with AI services layered in later.

Every slide must help prove that theorem. If it doesn't, cut it or move it to backup.

This is an evidence-light pre-seed deck. The product exists, but user evidence isn't yet available. Don't imply traction. Use the deck to sell the thesis, show the product, and define the proof the next round buys.

## Live pitch opening

Start with BLUF:

> Reflections helps people think clearly through private writing, then turns that trusted writing into long-term personal memory.

Then use the two-sentence layperson version:

> Reflections gives people a private place to write what they are thinking and return to what their writing is beginning to reveal. For example, someone can write through a difficult conversation today and later see the relationship pattern, mood rhythm, or decision trail without handing that context to a generic chatbot.

## Narrative spine

Use SCQA to keep the story tight:

| Step | Reflections version | Investor question |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Situation | People need private space to think, write, and return to what matters. | What human need does this start from? |
| Complication | That private context is scattered, forgotten, or handed to tools that have not earned trust. | Why does this create a new opening? |
| Question | Where will durable personal memory come from? | Why should this company exist? |
| Answer | Reflections starts with private writing, earns trust, lets memory develop, and uses Invited AI only after context exists. | Why this wedge and why now? |

## Paradigm shift opening

Open with the human shift before the technology shift:

> People are creating more private context than ever, but very little of it becomes memory they can trust and return to.

Do not open with:

> AI needs memory.

AI belongs in the timing argument. It should not be the protagonist.

## Assertion headline rules

Every slide headline must be a claim, not a label.

Use:

- Private writing is the trust mechanism for personal memory.
- Memory develops when honest writing compounds over time.
- AI becomes useful after trust and context exist.
- The next milestone is retention and conversion from memory features.
- This round funds proof, not polish.

Avoid:

- Product.
- Problem.
- Market.
- Competition.
- AI-first journal framing.

## Slide design rules

### Active Assertion-Evidence pairing

Each slide needs:

1. One sentence headline.
2. One primary proof object.
3. No more than four supporting chunks.

Good:

> Trust creates honest writing, richer context, better memory, and stronger retention.

Support it with a simple loop diagram.

Weak:

> Privacy and AI model

Followed by five bullets.

### Squint test

When blurred, the slide should still reveal its structure. A comparison should look like a comparison. A loop should look like a loop. A milestone should look like a timeline or scorecard.

### Four-chunk limit

Keep main slides to four or fewer visible chunks. Move dense details to backup.

### Evidence over bullets

Prefer screenshots, charts, diagrams, and tables. Use bullets only when they are the smallest clear proof.

## Evidence per slide

Each slide needs one visible proof object:

- A screenshot.
- A metric.
- A quote.
- A comparison table.
- A market sizing assumption.
- A diagram.
- A named experiment.

No slide should ask investors to accept a major claim on taste alone.

When user evidence doesn't exist, label the proof object as a plan, assumption, market proxy, screenshot, or experiment. Never present product screenshots as evidence of demand.

## Proof status labels

Use one of these labels for every major claim:

- Proven: supported by existing product, code, screenshot, or shipped capability.
- Observed: supported by market behavior, competitor pattern, public search/community signal, or founder experience.
- To be proven: requires user evidence, retention, conversion, interviews, or willingness-to-pay data.

If a claim is to be proven, make it part of the milestone narrative instead of stating it as fact.

## Thirty-day evidence sprint

Before serious investor outreach, run a focused evidence sprint:

- Interview 10-15 target users.
- Recruit 20-50 beta users.
- Track onboarding source.
- Measure Week 1 retention.
- Measure notes written per active user.
- Measure Life Wiki and AI reflection usage.
- Ask willingness-to-pay questions.

The goal isn't to prove everything. The goal is to replace the weakest assumptions with early signal.

## Narrative tension

Use a light Duarte/Zuora pattern:

| What is | What could be |
| --- | --- |
| Private thoughts disappear into scattered tools. | Writing becomes memory the user can return to. |
| Journals preserve entries but rarely show what changed. | Reflections helps patterns emerge over time. |
| Generic AI can feel too exposed for intimate context. | AI waits until the user asks. |
| Privacy is often treated as a feature. | Trust becomes the condition for honest writing and retention. |

The deck should alternate between the current broken state and the better future Reflections makes believable.

## Attention resets

For a live investor meeting longer than 10 minutes, change format at least once:

- Move from story to product screenshots.
- Move from product to a short demo.
- Move from metrics to the red-team milestone.
- Move from deck to discussion of the ask.

Don't add theatrical resets. Use the shift that best supports the proof.

## Audience-as-hero rule

Don't make Reflections the hero. Make the investor believe users are the heroes: they write, remember, and understand with more ownership over their private context.

Use:

> Reflections helps users keep their own personal context close without giving it away.

Avoid:

> Reflections understands users better than they understand themselves.

## Pre-read policy

Send dense material before or after the meeting:

- Full market sizing.
- Detailed encryption flow.
- AI architecture.
- Competitor matrix.
- Full roadmap.
- Raw user research notes.

Keep the live deck for decisions: why now, why this wedge, why this team, what must be proven next, and what the round funds.

## Slides to cut

Cut or move to backup when a slide:

- Lists features without changing investor belief.
- Repeats the thesis without new proof.
- Implies traction or user demand that doesn't exist.
- Makes a broad wellness claim.
- Leads with AI before explaining writing, trust, and memory.
- Uses market size to compensate for weak customer pain.
- Explains implementation detail before the investor understands why it matters.

## Backup slides

Keep backup slides for:

- Detailed AI architecture.
- Detailed encryption flow.
- Full pricing logic.
- Product roadmap.
- Full competitor matrix.
- User research notes.
- Metrics definitions.
